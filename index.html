Part I

Foundations: P-values and Error Rates

Every method rests on one idea: a valid p-value satisfies the super-uniform property \(\Pr_{H_0}(p \leq t) \leq t\) for all \(t\in[0,1]\). When many hypotheses are tested, controlling false positives requires understanding how p-values behave collectively.

Under the null, p-values are uniform. With alternatives present, the histogram develops excess mass near zero.

FWER

$$\text{FWER} = \Pr(V \geq 1)$$

Probability that any true null is falsely rejected. Bonferroni and Holm control FWER.

FDR

$$\text{FDR} = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{V}{\max(R,1)}\right]$$

Expected false discovery proportion. BH controls FDR. Standard in large-scale testing.

Key: When all nulls are true (\(m_0=m\)), FDR = FWER. FDR is less conservative only when some nulls are actually false.

Part III

Multiple Testing: BH & Holm

Holm (FWER) is step-down: stops at first failure. BH (FDR) is step-up: the more you reject, the more you allow. Their thresholds are \(\alpha/(m+1-k)\) and \(\alpha k/m\).

Part IV

Adaptive BH & Estimating \(\pi_0\)

BH controls FDR at \(\alpha m_0/m\), not \(\alpha\). Storey's estimator recovers the lost power.

Storey's \(\hat\pi_0\) Estimator

Amber bars (above \(\gamma\)) estimate \(\pi_0\). Blue bars (below \(\gamma\)) are rejection candidates.

Lecture 5: \(\hat\pi_0(\gamma)=(1+\#\{p_i>\gamma\})/[m(1-\gamma)]\). Adaptive BH at \(\alpha/\hat\pi_0\). The +1 prevents division by zero.

Formal statement

$$\hat\pi_0(\gamma) = \frac{1 + \#\{i : p_i > \gamma\}}{m(1-\gamma)}, \qquad \text{Adaptive BH level:}\;\alpha' = \frac{\alpha}{\hat\pi_0(\gamma)}$$

Under independence, \(\mathbb{E}[\pi_0/\hat\pi_0]\leq 1\), yielding FDR \(\leq\alpha\). Under strong dependence (e.g. \(p_1=\cdots=p_m\)), this can fail.

Permutation & Randomization Tests

When the null distribution is intractable, permutation tests construct it via exchangeability. The p-value is the rank of the observed statistic among permuted counterparts.

Part VI

Selective Inference & Knockoffs

Choosing what to test after seeing data invalidates classical inference. Two modern fixes:

Post-Selection Inference

Lectures 10–11
Conditional Coverage

Lasso's selection event is a polyhedron \(\{y:Ay\leq b\}\). Conditional on it, \(\eta^\top Y\) follows a truncated Gaussian.

$$\hat\beta_S = (X_S^\top X_S)^{-1}(X_S^\top Y - \lambda\, t_S)$$

Three fixes: data splitting, Bonferroni over all selections, or conditioning on the polyhedral event.

Model-X Knockoffs

Lectures 14–15
FDR Control

Construct pairwise-exchangeable fakes \(\tilde X_j\). Null variables and knockoffs are indistinguishable, so selected knockoffs are negative controls.

$$\widehat{\text{FDP}}(S) = \frac{\#\{j:\tilde X_j\in S\}}{\max(1,\,\#\{j:X_j\in S\})}$$

For \(X\sim N(\mu,\Sigma)\): augmented covariance \(\begin{pmatrix}\Sigma&\Sigma-D\\\Sigma-D&\Sigma\end{pmatrix}\). Each null coin flip is Bernoulli(1/2), giving FDR control without independence.
